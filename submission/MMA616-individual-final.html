Edmonton Rainfall & Storm Severity — an agentic workspace for drainage-operations duty supervisors

MMA 616 Individual Final · Neal Liu · dichuan@ualberta.ca · submitted July 2026
This workspace lets a drainage operations duty supervisor (EPCOR Drainage Services / City of Edmonton), during an active summer storm, decide where to send crews, which underpasses to barricade, whom to notify, and what to stand down — in the next 30 minutes — by turning 23 raw rain-gauge point readings into ranked severity, action-band counts, storm shape, and a building-vs-easing trend that the raw feed cannot show.

1 · The workspace

The workspace is a Claude Code project organized around a CLAUDE.md, connected to the City of Edmonton open-data feed 24 Hour Rainfall Totals and Storm Severity Ratings (x4rm-mppc) as a live SODA API source, with three project skills, one critic subagent, an /evaluate pre-ship workflow, and a single-file live HTML dashboard (a four-tab workspace) as the demonstration interface.

The living spec — CLAUDE.md

  • Two personas. The AI is a drainage operations analyst assistant with six operating rules (data currency first; never invent history; silence is a blind spot, not calm; speak in the action framework; respect the gauge-location caveat; verify before asserting). The user is the duty supervisor, whose recurring decision is spelled out verbatim.
  • Action framework. The feed's 0–8 storm rating is mapped to supervisor actions: 0–2 Normal · 3 Monitor · 4 Dispatch · 5 Barricade · ≥6 Escalate, quoting the feed's own consequence descriptions.
  • Seven testable metrics the dashboard must compute (citywide alert level, band counts, peak rainfall, spatial spread, per-station trend, gauge freshness, data-as-of stamp) — these are the success criteria the evaluation recomputes independently.
  • Non-negotiable data rules (exact endpoint, string-numeric parsing, the freshness trap, silence ≠ zero, off-season behaviour) — each one earned by a real failure found during use.

Knowledge — the dataset connected live

  • Primary source: https://data.edmonton.ca/resource/x4rm-mppc.json?$$exclude_system_fields=false&$limit=100 — fetched live by the dashboard every 5 minutes and by the skills on demand; nothing is pasted in.
  • knowledge/edmonton-rainfall-library.md — the dataset library: grain (one row = one gauge's current snapshot; the feed overwrites in place and keeps no history), every field with its type quirks, the rating scale, and the data cautions.
  • knowledge/research/edmonton-domain-context.md — why the decision matters (finite crews, underpass flooding as a life-safety risk, the 2004 design storm).
  • Companion live sources merged as context: planning districts (avbh-ga6n) and neighbourhoods (65fr-66s6), storm/combined pump stations (yhez-gf32), storm-water management ponds (72ee-mmkx), live road closures (k4tx-5k8p), 311 service requests (q7ua-agfg), EPCOR's live water-outage feature services (ArcGIS — discovered from the public app config, drawn as a native map layer), City of Edmonton traffic cameras, Environment Canada citypage forecast, Open-Meteo (hourly model + ERA5 archive), and ECCC Blatchford historical precipitation (s4ws-tdws) for the Stats charts; the Windy.com embed provides the regional radar/forecast animation (it replaced an earlier in-app ECCC GeoMet WMS map). Candidate datasets were evaluated and rejected (hourly ECCC weather: no precip field + corrupt dates; river water levels: stale/upstream; EPCOR pond/sewer telemetry: internal SCADA, not public) — recorded so the agent doesn't re-propose them.

Governance

  • Review points: every dashboard edit must pass dashboard-verify; before a demo/submission the dashboard-critic subagent runs and its findings are triaged by me.
  • Decides alone vs escalates: the agent computes and formats; it may not invent thresholds beyond the action framework, may not present stale data as current, and must quote the feed's own wording for consequences. Trend claims must state buffer depth.
  • Honesty mechanisms baked into the output: the data-as-of stamp is always visible; a missing gauge renders as a flagged blind spot, never 0 mm; session trends are labeled "this browser only — not official history"; feed failure shows "DATA UNAVAILABLE — do not assume no rain".

5 · Write-up

The user and the work. During Edmonton's May–October storm season, an EPCOR drainage duty supervisor repeatedly makes one call: given where it is raining hardest and how the sewer network is coping, where do crews go, what gets barricaded, who gets notified — in the next 30 minutes. The raw open-data feed is 23 independent point rows that overwrite in place: it cannot rank severity, count action thresholds, show whether a storm is a localized cell or citywide, or say if it is building or easing. Those four judgments — plus how much the data can be trusted right now — are exactly what the workspace computes, and nothing else. That restraint is deliberate: the strongest design decision was what to leave out (no neighbourhood interpolation the 23-gauge network cannot support, no "history" the snapshot feed does not keep).

How it is built. The CLAUDE.md is the contract: personas, the action framework in the feed's own language, seven testable metrics, and data rules earned from real failures. Knowledge is connected live, with a metadata library the agent must consult before touching the feed — the library exists because the dataset is full of traps that produce silently wrong numbers (string numerics, a timestamp that resets at midnight, an API flag required to see the real freshness stamp). The repeatable work is packaged as skills: a briefing generator for the supervisor's question, a metadata library for the agent's queries, and a verification harness that must pass after every edit; a critic subagent reviews from the supervisor's chair before anything ships. The dashboard demonstrates the workspace live as a four-tab interface: Station data (alert posture, an ordered priorities queue, duty tiles, a historical 311 heatmap, and the severity-ranked station table with a per-gauge ±12 h forecast column), Maps (a district choropleth with tick-to-show gauge / 311 / EPCOR-incident / pond / pump / closure / camera layers and a multi-select filter panel), Weather forecast (Environment Canada's 7-day forecast + the Windy.com regional animation), and Stats (multi-year precipitation, 311 open-vs-closed, category-composition, and EPCOR combo charts under one shared filter).

What the evaluation showed. Testing output rather than code found five real defects, and each had the same signature: the system looked right while being wrong (a plausible-looking string sort, a plausible-looking midnight timestamp, cameras returning HTTP 200 placeholders). The fix in every case was to recompute independently and encode the lesson as a rule the agent reads before it works. The before-and-after (5/10 → 10/10 on the verification suite) is less important than the pattern it proved: with a live, quirky feed, honesty features — freshness stamps, blind-spot flagging, session-local trend labels — are not polish, they are the product.

6 · Distinctness statement

Dataset and problem chosen: City of Edmonton open data, 24 Hour Rainfall Totals and Storm Severity Ratings (x4rm-mppc) — a live, self-overwriting, 23-station real-time snapshot feed — supporting a drainage duty supervisor's next-30-minutes dispatch/barricade/escalate decision during active storms. (Instructor confirmed a non–Government-of-Alberta open dataset is acceptable for this final.)

How it differs from the group project: my group's project was built on the Calgary 311 service-request dataset — a large historical archive of citizen-reported records analyzed retrospectively for service-delivery patterns. This final differs in jurisdiction and publisher (Edmonton vs Calgary), in domain (physical storm-sewer operations vs municipal service requests), in data shape (a live snapshot that keeps no history and must be buffered client-side vs a deep historical table), and in the decision it supports (a real-time operational dispatch call measured in minutes vs retrospective service analysis). None of the group's queries, knowledge files, or skills carry over; the Calgary exemplar files kept in this repo are explicitly fenced off in CLAUDE.md as "a different dataset — do not apply their rules here".
